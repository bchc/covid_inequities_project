Introduction

The World Health Organizations and the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention recommends using face masks to slow the spread of COVID-19 virus. However face coverings have not been as widely adopted in the US during this current epidemic as it has in previous epidemic epidemics (1918 Influenza Pandemic) or as in other countries internationally. Data on face mask usage is limited but the global survey firm Dynata at the request of The New York Times conducted a relative larg scale 250,000 survey between July 2 and July 14 to provide county level estimates of mask usage. The associated NYT article can be found here and the data can be found here.

Although, the NYT has no further plans to update the data or conduct the survey again this survey provides a potential glimpse to mask usage at a sub state level for at least two weeks in July. This post will use the Dynata mask usage data to not only compare the mask usage in BCHC cities but also to track association with longitudinal trends in COVID outcomes after the survey date.

Methodology

We operationalized the survey data into ‘Chance all five people are wearing masks in five random encounters’ as reported in the initial NYT analysis: “The chance all five people are wearing masks in five random encounters is calculated by assuming that survey respondents who answered ‘Always’ were wearing masks all of the time, those who answered ‘Frequently’ were wearing masks 80 percent of the time, those who answered ‘Sometimes’ were wearing masks 50 percent of the time, those who answered ‘Rarely’ were wearing masks 20 percent of the time and those who answered ‘Never’ were wearing masks none of the time.”
